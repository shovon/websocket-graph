# How a Flood Dies

## Status of This Memo

This document is **non-normative**. It owns the question the rest of the corpus keeps handing forward under the name *the loop-detection rule* — but it owns the question's *shape and its reasons*, never its numbers. `building-a-trail.md` puts a stop-test first in the handler and says only where it sits: "The slot is here. What fills it is not ours" (Trail §3). Emergence and Sending both lean, again and again, on a flood being a finite event, and both defer *how* it is made finite (Emergence §3, Sending §4). This memo is the place that deferral is examined rather than merely repeated. It asks what it even means for a flood to "terminate" — and argues that the single word hides three different things — and then walks three example mechanisms by which a node's *no* is reached. It fixes no wire format, mandates no field, names no number, and defines no conformance surface.

It is careful to own nothing else. It does not re-argue what a flood *costs*, or that one terminates as a global fact — that is Emergence's (Emergence §1, §3). It does not re-derive the three acts or the slate beneath them — that is Node's (Node §3). It does not re-place the stop-test in the handler — that is Trail's (Trail §3). It takes all of that as given and answers only the question those leave open: granted that a flood must die, *what does "die" mean, and by what kinds of local refusal is the dying bought?*

The register is the discursive one this corpus reserves for rationale. It is allowed to argue, to lean on a metaphor, and to repeat a load-bearing point on purpose. Nothing here uses RFC 2119 keywords; where it appears to instruct, read "this is the design the requirements were built to serve," not "you must." Section references resolve by name, not by location: (Trail §N) into `building-a-trail.md`, (Emergence §N) into `path-emergence.md`, (Node §N) into `what-a-node-is.md`, (Sending §N) into `sending-rule.md`, and (Completion §N) into `path-completion.md` — companions in this same folder.

## Abstract

A flood is the one tool the overlay has for reaching past a neighborhood, and it is a tool that must consume itself: a flood that does not die is not a flood, it is a broadcast storm (Emergence §1). So the interesting thing about a flood is not how it spreads — spreading is easy, it is just *send to every out-neighbor*, run everywhere at once — but how it *stops*. This memo is about the stopping. Its first claim is that "stopping" is not one property but three, run together by a word: a flood can stop **circulating** (no copy lives forever), stop **multiplying** (copies do not pile up, each node forwards each flood about once), and stop **spreading** (no copy travels past some bound) — and a rule can buy any one of these without the others. Its second offering is three examples of how a single node, looking only at the copy in its hand, reaches the *no* that ends a copy's travel: it can **remember** that it has carried this search before; it can **find itself already written into the copy's own record**; or it can read a **budget the copy carries** and see that it is spent. Each example buys a different one of the three guarantees, each pays a different price, and they compose. The memo commits to none of them, and to no number. Which to use, how an id is formed, how long a memory holds, what a budget counts down from — all of that is the normative layer's. This memo says only what *kinds* of stopping there are, and what *kinds* of refusal can produce them.

## 1. A flood is born to die

Begin with the asymmetry, because it is the whole reason this memo exists. Spreading a flood is trivial: a node sends a copy to every out-neighbor, every node does the same, and the substrate's one move — hand a message to an out-neighbor (Emergence §2) — carried out everywhere at once, *is* the flood. No node plans it, no node oversees it; it is the most automatic thing in the system. If spreading were all a flood did, there would be nothing to write down.

But a graph has cycles, and a flood loosed on a graph with cycles does not naturally stop. Follow the copies: one you forward to a neighbor reaches one of *its* neighbors, and around some loop returns to you; or a sibling copy of the same search, launched the same instant down a different edge, arrives from the far side. Either way a copy of a search you have already passed on is back in your hand, and if you forward it you have made a second wave from a search that was already spent. Do that at every node and the copies never decrease — the flood does not fan out and die, it *circulates*, and the network fills with discovery that carries no content. That is the broadcast storm, the failure mode the entire scheme is built to avoid (Emergence §1). So a flood that spreads for free must be made to die at some cost, and the death is the engineering.

Here is the shape of that death, and it is the same shape as the system's other deep fact, turned inside out. Node §1 draws a fault line: connectivity is a *global* property, visible only from the center, so it is the center's to keep. Termination is the mirror image. Whether a flood has died is also a global fact — it is a statement about the whole wave, that nowhere is any copy of it still in motion — and yet, unlike connectivity, it is a global fact that must be *bought entirely with local refusals*. No authority calls time on a flood. There is no node that can see the wave and declare it over. The flood dies only because, copy by copy, individual nodes each look at the one copy in hand and decline to forward it. Its death is as decentralized as its life: it ends everywhere and nowhere, the way it began (Sending §4). The center owns the global guarantee that *cannot* be made local; termination is the global outcome that *can only* be made local, and is — one *no* at a time.

So the unit of this memo is the single refusal: one node, one copy, the decision to drop it rather than pass it on. Everything below is about that decision — what it can be aiming at, and what it can be made of.

## 2. Three things "it stops" can mean

Now the claim the memo is really for. "Make the flood terminate" sounds like one request. It is three, and they are worth prising apart, because a mechanism can satisfy one and leave the others wide open, and a reader who has not separated them will think a rule sound when it has bought only a third of what was wanted.

**It stops circulating.** The weakest and most essential: no copy of the flood travels forever. Every copy, eventually, meets a node that drops it, so the flood is a *finite event in time* — it begins, it propagates, it is wholly gone. This is the floor. Below it lies the broadcast storm; without this, nothing else matters, because the network never gets its capacity back. Call this **finiteness**.

**It stops multiplying.** Stronger, and the thing you actually want. A flood can be finite and still ruinous: imagine copies that each die *eventually* but, before dying, each spawn a fresh fan-out, so that the *number* of copies in flight grows toward the number of paths through the graph — which is exponential — even though no single copy lives forever. Finiteness is satisfied; the bill is not. What a usable flood needs is that copies do not pile up: that each node forwards each flood *about once*, so the flood touches each edge roughly once rather than once per path that reaches it. This is the property that makes a flood cost $O(E)$ and not $O(\text{paths})$ — the durable, affordable flood Emergence's whole argument assumes (Emergence §1). Call this **efficiency**. It implies finiteness; finiteness does not imply it.

**It stops spreading.** Different in kind from the other two, and orthogonal to both. Independent of whether copies circulate or multiply, a flood can be made unable to travel *past some distance* — a hard cap on reach, owed to no one's memory and no graph's shape. A reach-bounded flood might still multiply wildly *within* the bound; a finite, efficient flood might have no reach bound at all and simply die when its work is done. The two say nothing about each other. Reach-bounding earns its keep precisely when the other two cannot be relied on — when you want a guarantee that holds *regardless* of whether any node's memory survived or the topology behaved. Call this **bounded reach**.

The payoff of separating them is the next section. Three guarantees, and — not by coincidence — three example refusals, each of which buys one of them squarely and the others only by accident or not at all.

## 3. Three ways a copy meets its *no*

Here are three mechanisms, offered as *examples* and not as a menu to choose one from. Each is a different thing a node can look at, in the copy it holds, to reach the *no* that ends that copy's travel. Read each against §2: what does it actually guarantee, and what does it leave open?

**The remembered *no*.** The node keeps, in itself, a record of the searches it has already carried — and when a copy arrives whose search it recognizes there, it drops it. This is the *have-I-seen-this* reading, the thin slate Trail §4 sketches ("enough to not forward the same flood twice") and one of the stop-predicates Node names outright (Node §3, "I have seen this already"). What it buys is **efficiency**, cleanly: it kills the returning copy *and* the converging sibling, because both belong to a search the node has logged, so each node forwards each flood about once and no more. It is the only one of the three that reaches efficiency at all. What it costs is the node's own memory — a set that grows with the number of distinct floods seen and that must be aged out, because a memory that never forgets grows without bound (Completion §3). And it has a precondition the other two share only in part: to recognize "this search," the search must be recognizable, which is §4's subject.

**The self-in-the-record *no*.** The node keeps nothing. It looks instead into the copy's *own accumulating trail* — the record of hops the copy has crossed (Trail §4) — and asks a single question: *is my own mark already in here?* If it is, then this very copy has passed through the node before, and the node drops it. This refusal is stateless: a pure read of the packet, no slate at all, the "slate as thin as nothing" Node §3 allows. What it buys is **finiteness** and only finiteness. A copy's trail grows by one hop each step, and this rule forbids a copy from ever revisiting a node already on its own path — so no copy can loop, and every copy's length is bounded by the number of nodes. The flood cannot circulate. But it can still *multiply*: two copies of one search arriving by two paths that do not yet contain this node both pass the test — neither has the node's mark — so the node forwards both, and the pile-up §2 warned of proceeds unchecked. This refusal catches the *cycle* and misses the *convergence*. It is cheap and it is real, but it is a floor, not a ceiling.

**The spent-budget *no*.** Again the node keeps nothing, but this time it does not even read the trail. The copy is *born carrying a number* — a distance it is allowed to travel — and each node it passes through spends one of it; a copy that arrives already at zero dies in the hand that holds it. This is the other materialization Node names (Node §3, "a budget the message carries"; "a budget is spent"). What it buys is **bounded reach**, by fiat, and as a consequence of bounded reach it buys finiteness too — a flood that cannot travel past $k$ hops plainly cannot circulate forever. What it does *not* buy is efficiency: within the budget, copies multiply exactly as freely as they like, and worse, the budget is blind to *progress* — it will cut down a copy that was still doing honest work right alongside one that was merely going in circles, because it cannot tell the two apart. Its virtue is exactly its bluntness: it asks nothing of the node's memory and nothing of the search's identity, so it holds even when everything else has failed — a memory evicted too soon, a search that was not recognizable, a copy replayed by a node that does not keep the contract. It is the guarantee of last resort, and it owes its strength to depending on nothing.

Now the thing the three examples are arranged to show. They are not rivals; they are *layers*, because they buy different guarantees (§2). The natural shape — the one the separation in §2 all but draws for you — is to take **efficiency** from the remembered *no* in the common case, and to keep a **budget** underneath it as the floor that holds even when the memory is lost or gamed, so that *finiteness never depends on soft state surviving*. The self-in-the-record *no* sits between them, free and subsumed: efficiency covers what it covers for cycles, the budget covers termination if memory is gone, and it costs nothing to keep as a third line. Whether to layer them so, or to lean on one alone, is a real choice with real consequences — and it is the normative layer's, not this memo's (§5). What this memo insists on is only that the chooser know which guarantee each piece is *for*, because the worst rule is the one that feels finished having bought finiteness and believing it bought efficiency.

## 4. What recognition asks of the search

Two of the three examples — the remembered *no* and, in a narrower way, the self-in-the-record *no* — work by *recognizing* something, and recognition is not free. It places a demand on the search itself, and the demand is the same one the old framing of this branch turned on, so it is worth keeping even though termination, not recognition, is this memo's spine.

To drop a copy *for being one it has already handled*, a node must be able to tell which search a copy belongs to — and two things a naive reading runs together have to come apart.

First, two different searches for the *same destination* are not the same search. Node $A$ looking for $B$ and node $C$ looking for $B$ are distinct floods; a node that conflated them would drop $C$'s copies because it had logged $A$'s, and $C$'s search would die at every node $A$'s had touched. So "the same search" cannot mean "same target." It needs a mark that distinguishes *this* flood from every other — a per-flood handle, fixed at origination — and the search must carry that mark, unchanged, on every copy. (And note the same demand bites a step further out, the place this corpus keeps for later: two *successive* searches by the same origin for the same target — the first, and the re-flood after it went unanswered — are also distinct floods, and must not be recognized as each other, or the second dies network-wide before it starts. Whatever the handle is, it must turn over between them. That this is so belongs here; what the handle is made of does not.)

Second, the mark must be the part of the copy that does *not* change in flight. A copy is transformed at every hop — its trail grows (Trail §4) — so whatever identifies the search has to be the invariant, recognizable on the copy that arrived the long way around exactly as on the one that came direct. The growing trail is precisely *not* it. (The self-in-the-record *no* reads that growing trail too — but for a different purpose: not to ask *which search is this*, but *am I already in it*. It needs no per-flood handle at all, which is why it can be stateless; it is recognition of *self*, not of *search*.) A node that keys on the invariant ignores the rest.

What the invariant is *made of* — how a handle is formed, how wide it must be for collisions to be negligible, and whether it should be opaque to the carriers that must read it so later hardening can blind them to who is searching — is exactly what this memo must not decide, because deciding it would foreclose, quietly, a choice the requirements should make in the open. What belongs here is only that *some* per-flood invariant must exist and ride every copy, or two of the three examples have nothing to recognize.

## 5. What stays the normative layer's

This memo drew the concept of termination and three examples of the refusal that produces it, and stopped at the edge of every number on purpose. Everything that turns the concept into a wire-level rule is deferred, and the deferral is the same one the rest of the corpus makes (Emergence §3, Sending §4, Node §3): the precise rule by which a node drops a copy is the normative companion's to pin down, and assuming it settled is what lets every memo above it reason cleanly.

Concretely, what is *not* decided here: which of the three examples to use, or in what layering; how a per-flood handle is formed and how wide it must be; whether the handle must be opaque to carriers, and how; how long a remembered entry must be retained before it is safe to forget; what a budget's initial value must be to cross the graph without licensing a flood that circles within it. Those are numbers and formats, and numbers and formats are what a non-normative memo must refuse to invent.

What *is* decided, and is this memo's to hand upward: that a flood must die, and that its death is a global outcome bought only with local refusals (§1); that "die" names three separable guarantees — finiteness, efficiency, bounded reach — and that a rule may buy one without the others, so a chooser must know which it is buying (§2); that there are at least these three kinds of refusal a node can reach from the copy in its hand, that they buy different guarantees and compose into layers rather than competing (§3); and that any refusal which works by *recognition* demands a per-flood invariant, carried unchanged on every copy and distinct from both the destination and the growing trail (§4). Fill the slot however the requirements decide — but it is these the filling has to honor, and the one option ruled out is the empty slot, because an empty slot is a flood that never dies.
