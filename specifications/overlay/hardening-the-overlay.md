# Hardening the Overlay

## Status of This Memo

This document is **non-normative**. It specifies nothing, requires nothing, and conforms to nothing. It is the last memo of the overlay set, and the one to read once the others have done their work — ideally when you are no longer merely understanding the overlay but preparing to implement one fit to deploy.

The mechanism memos (`path-emergence.md`, `path-completion.md`, `sending-rule.md`) show a node reaching a non-neighbor and getting an answer back, and they do it in a deliberately perfect world: every node honest, every name simply granted. This memo is where that world ends. It is the **hardening** — what you add, and why, once you stop assuming that nobody lies, drops, or snoops. None of it changes the dance the mechanism memos describe; all of it bolts on. That is exactly why it waits until here: pulling it forward burdens the mechanism with weight it need not carry to be understood, and the corpus is deliberately built so that you can follow the whole scheme without any of it. Read this when you are ready to leave the perfect world — and not before.

When you want to know *why the choices implementation forces take the shape they do* — why identity ends up cryptographic, why authentication is end-to-end, why the envelope is layered, why carriers are trusted with nothing — you come here. Cite this document for reasoning, never for conformance. Nothing in it uses RFC 2119 keywords, and where it appears to recommend, read "this is the judgment the implementation should weigh," not "you must."

A word on register, since it is the document's whole reason for existing: a conformance spec is prose pressed into service as code, and it pays for its precision in readability. That price is worth paying where interoperability is at stake and not a penny elsewhere. This memo is the elsewhere. It is allowed to argue, to hedge, to repeat itself for emphasis, and to address a human rather than a parser. If it reads like an essay, that is not a lapse; it is the assignment.

Section references resolve by name, not by location. Those of the form (Architecture §N) point into `../architecture.md`, (Relay §N) into `../relay-and-neighborhood-semantics.md`, (Designator §N) into `../designator-string.md`, and (Payload §N) into `../payload-string.md` — substrate documents that sit one level up from this folder. Companion overlay memos in this same folder are cited by filename.

## Abstract

The GRS relay hands clients a deliberately impoverished primitive — send to an out-neighbor, hop by hop, with no broadcast, no reply path, no sender attribution, and no acknowledgement (Architecture §3.1, Relay §6). Everything an application actually wants — reaching a non-neighbor, getting an answer back, knowing who it is talking to — must be constructed *above* that primitive (Architecture §3.2). The mechanism memos build exactly that, in a perfect world. This memo is what the real world adds back: the hardening that becomes necessary the moment those perfect-world assumptions fail, organized around four decisions that interlock — **self-certifying identity**, **end-to-end authentication**, **the untrusted-carrier stance**, and **a layered, extensible envelope** — together with the order in which they have to be made and the boundary between what hardening must settle and what it can still defer. They are not four independent good ideas. They are one decision with four faces, and the thesis of this memo is that, once you do harden, making it once and together is what lets nearly everything else wait.

## 1. The ground we are standing on

Start by being honest about what the substrate refuses to do, because every decision downstream is a response to one of these refusals.

The relay hands a receiver no origin it can build on. The substrate defines no sender field, so the overlay assumes none arrives — and even were one to arrive, an untrusted carrier's word for who sent a thing is worth nothing. Who-sent-this is therefore the overlay's to establish, not something it relies on the substrate to supply; whether a given substrate happens to reveal which edge a message came in on is simply not our concern. The relay will not carry a reply: an in-edge is not a send path (Architecture §3.1), and the only nodes you can reach are your current out-neighbors, full stop, replies and acknowledgements and errors included. The relay will not confirm delivery; acceptance is not delivery and silence means nothing (Relay §6). And the names it does hand you — designators — are good only within a single neighborhood state, reusable and unstable from one moment to the next (Designator §5); they are handles for *addressing the next hop right now*, not identities for *anything across time*.

What it *does* promise is thin but exactly enough: the graph stays strongly connected, so a directed path from any node to any other always exists (Architecture §3). A flood following out-edges therefore reaches everyone. That single guarantee is the floor the entire overlay is built on, and it is worth noticing how little it is. It says a path *exists*. It says nothing about your finding it, about anyone cooperating to carry your traffic along it, or about your ever hearing back. Reachability is a property of the graph, not a service offered to you.

So the overlay's job is to manufacture, out of "a path exists," the things the substrate withholds: a way to name the party you want, a way to know a message really came from them, a way to answer, and a way to do all of this while standing on carriers you have no reason to trust. The next four sections are those manufactures, in dependency order.

## 2. Identity has to be ours, and it has to be cryptographic

The first thing you reach for when you want to address `b` is a name for `b`. The substrate offers designators, and they are the wrong tool — not weak, *wrong*. A designator names an out-neighbor in a particular neighborhood state and may denote a different node, or nothing, a moment later. You cannot say "the `b` I spoke to yesterday," you cannot put `b` in a message and have a distant carrier know which node you mean, and the spec forbids you even from trying to read structure into the string (Designator §3). Designators are addresses of *positions*, and you need names for *parties*.

So the overlay mints its own. Each participant generates a keypair and is known by its public key (or a hash of it). The server neither provides this nor cares about it; it lives entirely above the relay, exactly where Architecture §3.2 says such things belong. This is the "self-certifying identifier" — self-certifying because the name *is* the verification key: anyone holding the name can check a signature made under it, with no authority to ask and no registry to consult.

It matters that this is cryptographic and not merely a random string everyone agrees to use as a label, and the reason is the whole rest of the document. A cryptographic name gives you **authenticity for free**: a message that verifies under `b`'s key demonstrably came from whoever holds `b`'s private key, no matter how many untrusted carriers it crossed to reach you. That property — *I can believe this came from the named party even though I trust nothing in between* — is the hinge on which §3 and §4 turn. A non-cryptographic label gives you none of it, and everything downstream would have to be rebuilt to add it. So identity is cryptographic from the first line, not because we are security-minded by temperament, but because the cheap defenses of the entire overlay are all denominated in signatures, and you cannot sign under a name that is not a key.

Two honesty notes, because self-minted identity is seductive and oversold. A self-certifying name proves *non-impersonation* and nothing else. It does not prove scarcity — minting is free, so one actor can be a million names (this is Sybil, and §6 parks it). And it does not solve *rendezvous*: to flood "I want to reach `b`" you must already hold `b`'s key, and how you came to hold it — introduction, directory, prior contact — is a separate problem this overlay does not answer. The name certifies the party; it does not introduce you to them.

## 3. The pivot: authenticate end to end

Here is the single decision that earns its keep more than any other, and the one that, skipped, makes a flag day out of every later improvement.

Every message the overlay originates is **signed end to end** — by the originator, under its self-certifying key, over the message's meaningful content — and, where confidentiality is in scope, encrypted to the recipient the same way. Not hop by hop. Not by the relay. By the endpoints, for the endpoints, verifiable only against names that §2 made cryptographic.

Why this is *the* pivot: it collapses the power of a misbehaving carrier down to a single verb. Once the content is authenticated end to end, a carrier can drop your message, stall it, duplicate it, or watch it go by — but it cannot forge one, cannot alter one undetectably, and cannot impersonate the party you care about. The entire space of carrier attacks shrinks to **denial**. And denial, it turns out, is the one class of attack whose defenses are *additive* — redundancy, multiple paths, reputation, proof-of-work — things you bolt onto a working best-effort substrate later without disturbing it. Forgery and impersonation are not deferrable; they would poison everything from the bottom. Denial is deferrable. So the work of this one decision is to convert the non-deferrable threats into deferrable ones, and that conversion is what the whole "settle a little now, defer the rest" story in §6 is paying for.

State it the other way to feel the stakes: ship a first version where the content is *not* authenticated end to end, and every cooperation defense you later want becomes a coordinated upgrade across an anonymous, churning population you cannot even enumerate — which §5 explains is not a hard upgrade but an impossible one. The end-to-end signature is the small thing you do now so that the large things can be done later, one at a time, by whoever wants them.

## 4. The untrusted-carrier stance

The stance is a posture, held everywhere and stated once: **treat every carrier as a conduit that moves your bits but whose honesty, correctness, and cooperation you assume nothing about. Trust lives only end to end, bound to the names of §2. Never hop by hop.** It is the security reading of the end-to-end argument, and it is the same stance TLS takes toward the network and a mix network takes toward its relays — "the network is the adversary," not because every relay *is* hostile, but because you decline to *depend* on any of them not being.

In practice it is a discipline for sorting everything the overlay handles into two bins. **Facts** are the only things you act on as true, and there are exactly two kinds: something signed end to end under the name that matters (§3), or a local fact a node checks about *itself* (the canonical one being "am I the intended next hop? — I check my own name against the route entry," which is a node verifying a claim about itself, not trusting a carrier). **Hints** are everything a carrier supplies that you cannot verify end to end: the accumulated path it claims a probe took, its assertion that it forwarded your message, its report that a destination is unreachable, its offer of a wonderful short route. Hints are suggestions, never authority. You verify a route *by using it and seeing whether an end-to-end-authenticated answer comes home* — never by believing a carrier that says it is good.

The stance's great economy is that it collapses every carrier failure mode into one. Crashed, lazy, buggy, curious, or actively malicious — from where you stand these are indistinguishable and identical: *I did not get my authenticated answer.* You do not need to tell evil from dead, because the same end-to-end timeout-and-retry machinery handles both. That is why the stance is cheap to hold: it shrinks the threat model rather than enlarging it.

And it is just as important to say what the stance does *not* give you, because the boundary is exact and deliberate. It protects integrity and authenticity — a sealed message cannot be opened or its seal forged. It does **nothing** for availability. A carrier you do not trust can still simply refuse to carry, and the stance's only response to that refusal is to guarantee the refusal cannot *lie* to you — you will never mistake a dropped message for a delivered one, nor trust a false "I forwarded it." This is **non-deception, not delivery**. Refusal-to-forward is fully inside the stance as an *assumption* — you presume nodes may drop — and fully outside it as a *remedy*: actually getting through despite an uncooperative carrier is the availability work of §6, and the stance does not even promise to *detect* a refusal, since silence is ambiguous. It promises only that whatever does come back is real.

## 5. The envelope, and why it is layered

The overlay's framing — origin name, target name, flood nonce, hop budget, message type, accumulated path, signatures, and in time everything deferred — does not ride in the relay's wire format. It rides *inside* the relay's opaque payload, the string the server carries verbatim and never interprets (Payload §4). That inner structure is the **envelope**, a private convention among clients that the server has no idea exists.

The envelope must be able to grow, and the reason it must is sharper here than in ordinary systems. In an ordinary system, a rigid format means a painful coordinated upgrade. In this one, coordinated upgrade is not painful but *impossible*: the participants are anonymous, churning strangers you cannot enumerate, cannot reliably reach, and cannot schedule. There is no flag day available to call. So a format that cannot evolve **unilaterally and incrementally** cannot evolve at all, and "design it to grow" stops being hygiene and becomes the only door through which the protocol will ever change.

The way to make it grow is **layering** rather than a flat versioned field-map — and the difference is not cosmetic. A layer treats everything beneath it as an opaque body. That single property turns the disciplines we would otherwise have to *ask carriers to obey* into facts they *cannot violate*. "Ignore the parts you don't understand" becomes "you physically can't see them." "Don't mangle the sealed core" becomes "you can't read it, and the signature would catch you if you tried." Under the untrusted-carrier stance this is gold: it converts a trusted behavioral rule into a structural guarantee, which is exactly the upgrade §4 is always reaching for. There is a pleasing recursion in it, too — you are reusing on your own carriers the very trick the server uses on you: hand them an opaque inner thing they carry without understanding.

The envelope falls into two zones, and the layering makes the split natural instead of imposed. The **sealed core** — origin, target, nonce, budget, type, content (or its ciphertext), and the originator's signature over all of it — is the innermost layer, immutable and end-to-end authenticated, the home of §4's *facts*. The **mutable transit section** is what the journey accretes: each hop appends its annotation (its own name, the designator it forwarded along, perhaps a signature over just that entry), an outer layer wrapped on at each step. These are *hints*; they cannot be in the originator's signature because they did not exist when it signed. A carrier may only append to the transit zone and must pass the sealed core along untouched — which, because the core is opaque to it, it could not violate undetectably even if it wished.

Layering does not abolish versioning; it *relocates* it, and it is worth being precise that you still pay a little. Each layer's own header can still need a new field someday, so a version tag lives on each layer rather than on one monolith — finer-grained and cheaper, but still versioning. And the set of layer types needs a marker distinguishing *optional* extensions (an unknown one is opaque, so ignore-and-forward is free and safe) from *critical* ones (an unknown one means you would mishandle the message, so refuse rather than guess) — the moment that keeps a future "this is encrypted" layer from being silently skipped and its ciphertext treated as cleartext. Layering also adds one obligation a flat map never had: a composition *order*, because whether you sign-then-encrypt or encrypt-then-sign, and whether a per-hop proof sits inside or outside the signature, are security-consequential choices you now have to make on purpose. The slogan to keep: *layering composes the features; versioning evolves each one.* You want both.

## 6. The deferral boundary

Everything above converges on one practical payoff: a small set of decisions that must be made now, and a large set that can wait — with the first set chosen precisely so the second set stays additive.

**Settle now**, because skipping any of these forecloses the rest:

- **Cryptographic self-certifying identity** (§2) — needed for addressing anyway, and the keying material every later defense is denominated in.
- **End-to-end authentication of content** (§3) — the pivot; it is what makes carrier misbehavior collapse to denial and thus what makes the deferred set additive.
- **A layered, extensible envelope** (§5) — the only form of evolution the network permits; reserve the layer framing, type codepoints, and critical/optional marker now even though you implement zero extensions today.
- **The untrusted-carrier stance** (§4) — architectural, not mechanical; committing to it is free, and it dictates where every signature lives. Not committing plants invisible trust dependencies that surface later as redesigns.

Two cheap "design-for-the-future" gestures belong to *now* as well, because retrofitting them is dear: design **acknowledgements** — which §3 and §4 already make you need for liveness — generously enough to attribute a loss to a particular route, so later drop-detection can reuse them; and have discovery **keep more than one** of the routes it surfaces rather than discarding all but one, so multipath is cheap later instead of a fresh flood. And one thing is a *decision* now even though its build is later: whether **relationship-hiding** is in scope at all. It is the single deferral that leaks while you wait — a cleartext "`a` wants `b`" tells every carrier your social graph, and you cannot un-leak history — so the scope call must be made up front even if the encryption layer is added afterward.

**Defer freely**, because the now-set has made each of these something you add on top without reopening the design: Sybil resistance and admission control; selective-drop and black-hole detection, multipath, and reputation; flood-amplification rate-limiting and proof-of-work; per-hop signatures on path entries; and the full traffic-analysis apparatus of onion-encrypted intent, padding, and cover traffic. Each has a home already — §5's two zones tell you which layer it extends — and each fights only denial or only privacy, never the integrity that §3 nailed down at the start.

The shape of the whole bargain, in one line: a few sentences of commitment now — cryptographic names, an end-to-end signature, a layered envelope, a stance — buy the entire deferred roadmap; omit the signature alone and none of it defers at all.

## 7. A closing note on what this document is for

A reader steeped in conformance specs may find this memo loose, and the looseness is deliberate and load-bearing. The companions will be exact because machines and interoperability demand it of them; ambiguity there is a defect. Here the audience is a person trying to understand *why the exactness elsewhere is shaped the way it is*, and for that reader a paragraph that argues beats a clause that merely commands. The two registers are not rivals; they are a division of labor. Keep the requirements where they can be checked, and keep the reasoning where it can be understood, and let each be written in the language its job actually wants. This document is the second of those, and it makes no apology for reading like one.
